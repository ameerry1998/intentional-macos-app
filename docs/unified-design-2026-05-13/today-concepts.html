10 ways to show Routines and Tasks on one calendar

The problem: two orthogonal scheduling concepts need to share one calendar. Routines (recurring time scaffolding — "Deep Work weekdays 9-11 AM") answer when. Tasks (goal-driven sessions — "Outline blog post #3") answer what. 10 concepts below, each genuinely different. Each has pros, cons, and the situation where it shines vs. fails.

01

Background tint bands

routines whisper, tasks shout
Routines render as faint colored backgrounds behind the hour rows. Tasks layer on top as solid colored blocks. The user sees both simultaneously without toggling: the colored bands tell them "this hour is in your Deep Work routine," and the solid block on top tells them "and the specific task you're doing is Outline blog #3." When a task is in a routine, the routine sits behind it as backdrop. When a routine has no task, the band stands alone as an invitation to fill it.

02

Routine as wrapper container

explicit nesting, tasks live inside
Routines are drawn as dashed bordered containers with a labeled header — "DEEP WORK · 9 AM – 11 AM." Tasks live inside these containers as solid blocks. Tasks outside any routine container float standalone with no frame. This makes the parent-child relationship explicit, like nested folders. The visual hierarchy is unambiguous: anything inside a wrapper IS that routine; anything outside is freelance work.

03

Two-mode toggle (Schedule / Tasks / Both)

explicit mode switch, focus per mode
A toggle at the top of the calendar switches between three views: Schedule (only routines visible, used for setting up the weekly scaffolding), Tasks (only today's tasks visible, used for execution), and Both (overlay, same as Concept 1). Mental model: setup mode vs. work mode. Like Notion's "Calendar view" vs. "List view."

04

Spectrum bar (24h glance + detail)

a 24h mood meter above the calendar
A horizontal 24-hour bar at the top of the page shows today's routines as colored segments — like a music waveform of your day's structure. A NOW indicator slides along it. Below the spectrum sits the regular vertical calendar showing tasks. The spectrum bar is a glanceable mood/shape preview; the calendar below has the detail.

05

Left-edge time spine

a vertical color strip indicates routine boundaries
A thin (12 px) colored vertical strip on the LEFT of the calendar shows routines as continuous segments. The main calendar lane to the right shows tasks. The spine is like a multi-color "ruler" running down the time axis — at any hour, glance left to see which routine you're in. Compact, distinctive, doesn't compete with the main timeline.

07

Hatched-pattern routines

stripes = structural, solid = specific
Routines render with diagonal stripe (hatching) pattern fills. Tasks render with solid color fills. Pattern signals "structural / recurring." Solid signals "specific / today." The visual language is "scaffolding vs content" via texture. Bonus: accessible for colorblind users — the pattern is a non-color cue.

08

Routine pill strip above calendar

routines summarized as chips, calendar shows tasks only
A horizontal strip of routine chips above the calendar lists today's active routines (Morning · Deep Work · Workout · Wind Down). Each chip shows time range and glows when its routine is currently active. The calendar below shows ONLY tasks. The chips give you the "what routines do I have today" answer at a glance; the calendar gives you "when am I doing my work."

09

Glyph hour labels (emoji-as-routine)

a single emoji per hour tells the routine
Routine info compresses into a single glyph in the hour label column. "9 AM 🧠" = 9 AM during Deep Work. "5 PM 🏃" = 5 PM during Workout. "9 PM 🌙" = 9 PM during Wind Down. The calendar lane stays clean for tasks. Glyphs are fast to scan, instantly recognizable. The downside: limited info density per glyph.

10

Z-axis depth (parallax)

routines blurred behind, tasks crisp in front
A 3D depth effect: routines exist on a "background plane" — slightly blurred, lower saturation. Tasks live on a "foreground plane" — sharp, full saturation. The visual depth signals priority and time-scale: routines are the structure (back), tasks are the action (front). Hover any element to bring it forward and reveal detail.

My ranking and recommendation

All 10 concepts solve the same problem, but they encode different priorities. Here's the ranking by "intuitive at first glance + flexible for power use + implementable now":
★ #1Concept 1: Background tint bands. What's currently shipped in today.html (v4). Best ratio of clarity to simplicity. Don't replace; iterate. #2Concept 8: Pill strip above. If we want a cleaner calendar, this is the runner-up. Lets the calendar focus on tasks while keeping routines a single glance away. #3Concept 5: Left spine. Beautiful and compact, but requires legend nearby — works as a hybrid with Concept 1's labels. #4Concept 9: Glyph labels. Surprisingly delightful in small doses. Could work as an additive layer alongside Concept 1. #5Concept 3: Two-mode toggle. Worth keeping as an OPTION on top of Concept 1 — let power users hide one layer when needed. #6Concept 4: Spectrum bar. Could be a permanent header element across all tabs (Today, Plan, Settings) — "the day's shape at a glance." #7Concept 6: Stacked dual. Better as the Plan tab's primary view (week routines + selected-day tasks) than Today. #8Concept 2: Wrapper container. Conceptually right but visually heavy. #9Concept 7: Hatching. Best for accessibility mode — could be an Accessibility setting that swaps tints for hatches. #10Concept 10: Z-axis depth. Premium but ages fast; revisit when we have a designer / motion lead.

My pick: ship Concept 1 (already done in today.html v4), add Concept 8 as an alternate view the user can switch to from a calendar setting, add Concept 4 as a permanent header strip across the whole app. Three concepts working together, each pulling its weight.

Or: if you want to commit to a single visualization, pick from #1, #2, #8, or #10 — they're the four most distinct, and I'll build whichever you choose into the canonical today.html.
