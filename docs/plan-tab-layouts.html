Option A
Segmented Toggle
A toggle at the top switches between Guided and Chat modes. Both are equal-weight. The toggle remembers your last choice. Simple, clear, familiar (iOS-style).
+ Very clear that two modes exist. Familiar iOS pattern. Easy to switch. Remembers preference.
- Feels slightly "app settings"-like. Two separate UIs to maintain. No blending of the two approaches.

Option B
Landing with Choice Cards
A brief landing page with two equal cards. The user picks their planning method each time. Feels like a deliberate choice. Could remember last pick and pre-select it.
+ Very discoverable — impossible to miss either option. Feels intentional (pun intended). Good for first-time users.
- Extra click every time before you can start planning. Gets tedious for daily users. Uses up vertical space.

Option C
Unified Input
One big text field. Type a list of tasks → get a structured schedule. Type conversationally → get a coaching response. The AI figures out the intent. Quick actions below for common patterns. No mode switching needed.
+ Zero learning curve. No modes to discover. Feels natural and fast. Quick actions give structure without forcing it.
- Users may not realize they CAN have a conversation (looks like a form). Small models might struggle to distinguish list vs. chat intent. Less structured than the wizard.

Option D
Chat-First with Inline Blocks
Chat is the primary UI. When the AI suggests blocks, they appear as inline cards within the conversation — accept all, edit individual blocks, or keep chatting. Quick-start shortcuts at the top for users who don't want to type. The wizard is gone; everything flows through conversation.
+ Most natural and flexible. Inline block cards make the output feel integrated, not like a separate "step 2". Quick shortcuts bridge the gap for fast planners.
- Slower for power users who just want to type 3 tasks and go. Conversation history takes up space. Relies heavily on AI quality.

Option E
Tabs with Cross-Preview
Two top-level tabs (Quick Plan / Coach Chat). Whichever you're NOT on shows a small preview card at the bottom teasing the other mode. "Want help deciding? Chat with coach →" or "Ready to commit? Switch to Quick Plan →". This solves discoverability while keeping the default clean.
+ Each mode gets full space. The preview card is a gentle nudge without being intrusive. Tabs are universally understood.
- Two completely separate UIs. Preview card could be ignored (banner blindness). Slightly more complex than the segmented toggle.
